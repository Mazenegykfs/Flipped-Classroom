Using Colons in Technical Writing

1. Objectives

  • Use a colon to correctly introduce a list.
  • Use a colon to introduce an explanation, example, or important point.
  • Ensure the clause preceding a colon is a complete sentence.
  • Recognize the conventional uses of colons in titles, ratios, and time.

2. Review

A colon (:) is a punctuation mark used to signal that something will follow: a list, an explanation, an example, or a quotation. It directs the reader's attention to the information that comes after it.

3. Warm-up

How does the colon change the emphasis of this sentence?

1. The experiment had one catastrophic result the main computer exploded.

2. The experiment had one catastrophic result: the main computer exploded.

The colon in Sentence 2 creates a dramatic pause, emphasizing that the explosion was the "catastrophic result." It formally connects the cause and the effect, making the statement more powerful.

4. Presentation: Two Key Colon Rules

Video Lesson:

Rule 1: To Introduce a List

Use a colon after an independent clause (a complete sentence) to introduce a list of items.

Correct: The circuit requires three components: a resistor, a capacitor, and an inductor.

Incorrect: The required components are: a resistor, a capacitor, and an inductor. (The part before the colon is not a complete sentence).

Rule 2: To Introduce an Explanation or Example

Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a further explanation, a clarification, or a key example.

Example: The system has a critical flaw: it overheats under minimal load.

Example: The results pointed to one conclusion: the hypothesis was correct.

Lesson: Using Semicolons in Technical Writing

1. Objectives

  • Correctly connect two closely related independent clauses with a semicolon.
  • Use semicolons to separate items in a complex list that already contains commas.
  • Differentiate between the correct use of a semicolon, a period, and a comma.
  • Avoid common semicolon errors, such as the comma splice.

2. Review

An independent clause is a complete thought that can stand alone as a sentence. Both clauses joined by a semicolon must be independent.

Example: The calculations were finished.

3. Warm-up

How does the punctuation change the flow of these sentences?

1. The simulation failed. The server overheated.

2. The simulation failed; the server overheated.

Sentence 2 is more fluid. The semicolon shows a strong, direct connection between the two ideas (cause and effect), making the writing more sophisticated than two separate sentences.

4. Presentation: Two Key Semicolon Rules

Video Guide: Semicolon Usage

Rule 1: To Connect Related Independent Clauses

Use a semicolon to join two complete sentences that are closely related in thought. It's stronger than a comma but weaker than a period.

Example: The stress test was completed; the results were conclusive.

Incorrect: The stress test was completed, the results were conclusive. (This is a comma splice error).

Rule 2: To Separate Items in a Complex List

Use a semicolon to separate items in a list when the items themselves contain commas. This prevents confusion.

Example: The project team included members from Cairo, Egypt; Riyadh, Saudi Arabia; and Dubai, UAE.

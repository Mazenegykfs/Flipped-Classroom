Punctuation: Parentheses ( ) vs. Quotation Marks (“ ”)

1. Objectives

  • Differentiate between the function of parentheses and quotation marks.
  • Correctly use parentheses to enclose non-essential or supplementary information.
  • Correctly use quotation marks to indicate direct speech or to cite titles of short works.
  • Apply the correct rules for placing other punctuation marks inside or outside of parentheses and quotation marks.

2. Review

Parentheses ( ) are used to add extra, non-essential information that clarifies or elaborates on a point. They de-emphasize the text inside them. Quotation marks (“ ”) are used to show the exact words someone said or wrote.

3. Warm-up

How would you punctuate this sentence to show the speaker's exact words?

The project manager said the final design is approved.

Correct Version: The project manager said, “The final design is approved.”
Quotation marks are necessary to indicate that “The final design is approved” are the exact words spoken by the project manager.

4. Presentation: Parentheses vs. Quotation Marks

When to Use Parentheses ( )

Rule: Use to enclose extra information that is not essential to the main point of the sentence. This can include acronyms, dates, or asides.

The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) provides the guidelines.

The final report (due Friday) must be submitted to the client.

When to Use Quotation Marks (“ ”)

Rule 1 (Direct Quotes): Use to enclose the exact words of a speaker or a source.

The lead engineer stated, “We need to increase the safety margin.”

Rule 2 (Punctuation Inside): Commas and periods almost always go inside the closing quotation mark in American English.

He referred to the problem as a “minor setback.”
